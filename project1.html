Case Study — 01

MusicSurf

Designing an emotion-driven music discovery platform that lets users explore music like a forest — by mood, genre, and feeling.

Role UX Design & Research
Timeline Jan – Mar 2019
Context CPSC 481 · HCI I
Tools Adobe XD, Illustrator
MusicSurf

The Problem

At the time, no platform allowed users to discover music based on how they actually feel. Spotify and Apple Music surfaced music through auto-generated playlists and algorithm-driven recommendations — with no transparency, no emotional context, and no real sense of exploration. Users weren't in control of how they found new music.

The Solution

MusicSurf is a concept platform that uses visualization to create a more natural music browsing experience. It maps important elements of songs — mood, speed, energy, genre — spatially in a network graph, so users can explore music the way you'd wander through a forest: intuitively, emotionally, and freely.

Instead of playlists and albums, songs are connected to each other by heuristics. Users can follow a path based on how they feel, or let the platform guide them. The result is discovery that feels personal — not algorithmic.

Low-Fidelity Wireframes — Login, Graph View, Now Playing, Explore

Research & Process

This was a full end-to-end UX project completed as part of my Human Computer Interaction I course at the University of Calgary. The process included:

  • Stakeholder analysis and competitive review of existing music platforms
  • End-user interviews exploring how people discover and relate to music
  • Sketches and paper prototypes to explore the network graph interaction model
  • Low-fidelity wireframes for all core screens
  • High-fidelity prototype built in Adobe XD with full interaction flows
  • Peer and instructor critique sessions with iterated revisions
High-Fidelity Screens — Login, Library, Now Playing, Explore More

Design Decisions

The colour palette draws from Spotify's dark-theme conventions, using a rich colour range within the network graph itself to communicate mood and genre — warm colours for energetic songs, cooler tones for ambient and melancholic tracks. This created an immediately readable visual language without requiring any text labels.

Particular attention was paid to the "Now Playing" experience — surfacing mood and energy data directly in the interface so users could understand why a song felt the way it did, and seamlessly continue their journey from there.

Lessons Learned

Stakeholder and competitor analysis early in the process was invaluable — it sharpened the focus of the design and gave the team a clear point of difference to design toward. I also learned how to apply and respond to structured critique from peers and instructors, which became a critical part of how I refine work today.

If I rebuilt this project today, I'd spend more time on user testing with real participants and lean further into accessibility — especially around how colour is used to communicate emotion for users with colour blindness.
